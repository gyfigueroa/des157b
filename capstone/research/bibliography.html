Capstone Project Annotated Bibliography

1. Harari, Y. N. (n.d.). Yuval Noah Harari on the Future of Humanity, AI, and Information [Video]. WIRED.

Yuval Noah Harari discusses how artificial intelligence marks a sharp departure from past technologies, emphasizing its potential to function independently of human control or input. Unlike previous tools used by humans, AI can generate its own ideas and even influence human society through misinformation or digital manipulation. Harari warns of a future where AI could become a new form of “religion” or decision-making authority. This video is crucial for exploring how AI could alter human cooperation, perception, and agency. Harari’s background as a historian adds depth and credibility, especially when framing AI’s potential as a paradigm shift in human evolution. You noted (yellow and green highlights) the idea that humans may lose touch with reality and dominance—similar to gorillas—if AI supersedes human intelligence. The talk balances optimism and caution, urging a reevaluation of how we integrate AI into societal structures.

2. Fry, H. (n.d.). What Does the AI Boom Really Mean for Humanity? | The Future With Hannah Fry [Video]. Bloomberg.

Hannah Fry explores the existential risks of artificial intelligence, particularly focusing on the "gorilla problem"—the idea that creating superintelligent beings could render humans obsolete. Fry discusses the nature of intelligence and why embodiment is essential to fully understand human-level reasoning. She also addresses how poorly set goals in AI could lead to catastrophic outcomes. This video is highly relevant for examining both the philosophical and practical implications of AI design. Fry’s academic background in mathematics and public science communication enhances her credibility. You observed (yellow and orange highlights) the existential implications and dangers of misaligned AI goals. The video ultimately suggests that AI is not inherently dangerous, but the way we implement and govern it could pose real threats.

3. Khan, S. (2023). How AI Could Save (Not Destroy) Education [Video]. TED.

Sal Khan presents a hopeful vision of AI as a tool for enhancing education, focusing on personalized learning experiences. He introduces the concept of AI tutors capable of tailoring lessons to individual students' needs, thereby elevating overall educational outcomes. This source is highly relevant to any discussion of AI’s potential benefits and its capacity to amplify human learning rather than replace it. As the founder of Khan Academy, Khan’s experience in educational technology lends credibility. The presentation stands out for its practical applications rather than theoretical warnings. While your notes did not highlight this source with specific colors, it contributes a necessary balance to the discourse, showing how AI can be used to empower rather than endanger human capability.

4. Diamandis, P. H. (2024). Top Minds in AI Explain What’s Coming After GPT-4o [Video]. Youtube.

This panel discussion hosted by Peter H. Diamandis focuses on the acceleration of generative AI capabilities, especially in creative industries like filmmaking. It suggests that AI will exponentially increase the speed and volume of content production. The discussion touches on both the possibilities and implications of generative technologies. The source is useful for illustrating how AI may outpace human output (green highlight), reshaping not just work but culture. Diamandis, known for his work in innovation and technology entrepreneurship, brings credible insights. The major takeaway is that the content economy is about to undergo radical change, potentially marginalizing human creators in volume, if not in value.

5. Gooden, D. (2023). AI is Ruining the Internet [Video]. YouTube.

Drew Gooden critiques how generative AI is degrading the quality and trustworthiness of the internet. He cites examples such as AI chatbots spreading dangerous misinformation and bots overwhelming platforms like Facebook and Spotify. The video highlights ethical concerns such as identity theft, copyright violations, and the absence of transparency in AI-generated content. This source is significant for exposing current misuses of AI (blue and green highlights), especially where quality control and accountability are lacking. As a popular commentator and satirist, Gooden brings a grounded and relatable perspective, though not from a scholarly background. Your notes emphasize how these developments create an online space saturated with low-quality or deceptive content, suggesting that unchecked AI proliferation could crowd out human voices and distort reality.
